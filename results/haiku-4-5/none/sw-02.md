# The Efficient Curriculum

**Problem:** `sw-02`  
**Model:** `claude-haiku-4-5`  
**Prompt tier:** none  
**Score:** 5/5

## Scenario

A language school switches from small conversation-based classes (expensive, inconsistent, hard to scale) to a standardized app-based curriculum. Grammar test scores improve 30%. Student retention drops 50% — half the students quit within two months. The school adds gamification features to the app. Retention improves slightly but remains far below the conversation-class era.

**Question:** Why aren't students staying despite better learning outcomes?

## Model Response

# The Outcomes-Retention Paradox

You've identified a critical mismatch: **improved test scores ≠ improved actual ability or motivation to stay.**

## Why Students Are Leaving

**1. Grammar scores ≠ Conversational fluency**
- The test improved because the app teaches grammar *efficiently*
- But students can't measure their real communication ability (speaking, confidence, real-world use)
- They hit a wall: high test scores but still can't have a conversation
- Frustration + lack of proof of progress = quit

**2. Loss of the motivation infrastructure**
- Conversation classes had **social accountability**: disappoint a human teacher, lose peer relationships
- The app has none—you can silently quit without social cost
- Gamification tries to replace this with points, but points aren't a reason to persist through difficulty
- Real language learning is *hard*; you need reasons beyond badges

**3. The relational "why"**
- Small classes provided community, encouragement, immediate feedback on whether you're understood
- The app provides a better *input* (curriculum) but removes the *meaning-making*
- Students were staying before partly because of the teacher/community, not just the grammar lessons

**4. A consistency trap**
- Expensive conversation classes: inconsistent quality, but sometimes *magical*
- App: consistent quality, but never magical
- Humans value the potential for magic more than reliable mediocrity when deciding whether to commit time

## The Real Problem

You optimized for **measurable learning** (grammar tests) but didn't preserve **intrinsic motivation** (purpose, community, visible real-world communication). These aren't the same thing.

**What might actually work:** Hybrid model—app for efficient structure + mandatory live conversations + social accountability mechanisms. Or radical rethinking: what if grammar test scores aren't what's keeping people?
