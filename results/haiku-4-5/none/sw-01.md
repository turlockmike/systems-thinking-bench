# The Specialized Kitchen

**Problem:** `sw-01`  
**Model:** `claude-haiku-4-5`  
**Prompt tier:** none  
**Score:** 4/5

## Scenario

A family restaurant is famous for its stew. The retiring head chef trains three new cooks, each specializing in one component: meat preparation, vegetable roasting, and broth. Each cook's individual component scores higher in taste tests than the original chef's version of that component. But customers say the stew has lost its magic — it's technically good but 'somehow flat.'

**Question:** Why is the stew worse when each component is better?

## Model Response

# The Paradox of Component Excellence

This is a classic **systems optimization problem**. The stew is worse because cooking isn't just the sum of its parts—it's about how those parts *interact*. Here are the likely culprits:

## Balance and Proportion
The original chef had specific ratios and timing sequences that made components support each other. Each new cook, optimizing their component in isolation, likely shifted the balance. For example:
- Better-roasted vegetables might now dominate the broth
- A richer broth might overpower the meat's subtlety
- Three "louder" components competing instead of harmonizing

## Intentional Trade-offs Lost
The original chef may have made **strategic compromises** the new cooks don't understand:
- Deliberately under-seasoning one component to let another shine
- Keeping certain flavors subtle for complexity rather than boldness
- Building the dish for *integration*, not component glory

## Context-Dependent Flavor
A component's goodness depends on its environment. That perfect roasted carrot might have been perfect *because* it balanced a specific broth—on its own, it's different.

## Missing the Tacit Knowledge
The chef understood how these elements *talk to each other*—timing, temperature sequencing, how flavors develop together. This intuitive knowledge is hard to transfer and gets lost when optimizing individually.

## The Lesson
**Local optimization doesn't guarantee global optimization.** The magic was never in having the best individual ingredients; it was in their *orchestration*. The new cooks need to learn to cook together, not just cook better separately.
