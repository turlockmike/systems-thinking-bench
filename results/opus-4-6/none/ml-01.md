# The Reliable Route

**Problem:** `ml-01`  
**Model:** `claude-opus-4-6`  
**Prompt tier:** none  
**Score:** 5/5

## Scenario

A regional airline touts a 96% on-time departure rate. Three of their routes serve remote island communities with hospitals that depend on time-sensitive medical supply flights. Management is told to improve the overall on-time rate to 98%. After six months of effort, they hit 98.2%. Island hospital administrators report that medical supply delays have gotten worse, not better.

**Question:** How did on-time performance improve while medical delays got worse?

## Model Response

# The Aggregate Metric Masked a Sacrifice of the Most Vulnerable Routes

This is a powerful real-world illustration of several intersecting statistical and management pathologies.

## The Core Mechanism

The island medical routes likely represent a **tiny fraction** of total departures. Imagine the airline operates 500 daily departures, of which perhaps 5–8 serve the island hospitals. Those island flights are roughly **1–2% of the total.**

This creates a brutal arithmetic:

| | Before (96.0%) | After (98.2%) |
|---|---|---|
| Total daily flights | 500 | 500 |
| Late flights allowed | 20 | 9 |
| Island flights | 7 | 7 |
| Island flights late | 1 | 3 |
| Mainland flights late | 19 | 6 |

The island on-time rate **collapsed from ~86% to ~57%**, but because those flights are such a small share of the total, the overall number still rose. The mainland improvement *more than swallowed* the island deterioration.

## Why This Happens in Practice

Several reinforcing dynamics likely drove it:

**Resource reallocation.** To hit 98%, management shifted spare aircraft, priority maintenance slots, and crew buffers toward high-volume mainland routes where each improvement moves the aggregate needle the most. Island routes — already the hardest to operate on time due to weather, distance, and small-airport constraints — lost their backstop resources.

**Rational triage under Goodhart's Law.** When the *measure* (overall on-time %) became the *target*, it stopped measuring what mattered. A rational optimizer ignores the subgroup that contributes the least to the metric, even if it's the subgroup where punctuality has the highest human stakes.

**Possible cancellation gaming.** Many on-time metrics only count flights that *operated*. If marginal island flights were canceled outright rather than dispatched late, they may have disappeared from the denominator entirely — improving the reported rate while making actual supply delivery *even worse* than the delayed-flight numbers suggest.

## The Deeper Lessons

**1. Simpson's Paradox in action.** Performance in *every subgroup that matters* can worsen while the aggregate improves, if the mix shifts or resources are redistributed toward the dominant subgroup.

**2. Aggregate KPIs are ethically dangerous when subgroups have unequal stakes.** A 2-hour delay on a competitive mainland trunk route is an inconvenience. A 2-hour delay on a medical supply flight to an island hospital can be a matter of life and death. A single number treats these as identical.

**3. The fix isn't just statistical — it's structural.** The airline needed:
- **Disaggregated reporting** — separate on-time targets for island medical routes
- **Floor constraints** — "overall rate must improve *without any critical route falling below X%*"
- **Weighted metrics** — where flights are weighted by consequence, not just counted equally

This scenario is a textbook case of why **optimizing for a single aggregate number, without constraints protecting vulnerable subpopulations, can produce outcomes that are statistically successful and morally disastrous.**
