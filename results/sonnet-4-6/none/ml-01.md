## Scenario

A regional airline touts a 96% on-time departure rate. Three of their routes serve remote island communities with hospitals that depend on time-sensitive medical supply flights. Management is told to improve the overall on-time rate to 98%. After six months of effort, they hit 98.2%. Island hospital administrators report that medical supply delays have gotten worse, not better.

**Question:** How did on-time performance improve while medical delays got worse?

## Model Response

## The Aggregate Metric Hides a Distribution Problem

This is a textbook example of how optimizing for a single number can actively harm the thing you care about most. Several mechanisms work together here:

---

### The Core Mathematical Reality

The island routes represent maybe 3 out of hundreds of routes — a tiny fraction of total flights. Consider a simplified example:

| Route Type | Flights | Original On-Time | After "Improvement" |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mainland (970 flights) | 970 | 96.5% (936 on-time) | 99.8% (968 on-time) |
| Island (30 flights) | 30 | 80% (24 on-time) | 47% (14 on-time) |
| **Overall** | **1000** | **96%** | **98.2%** ✓ |

The math works perfectly. The aggregate improved substantially while the island routes collapsed.

---

### Why This Happens in Practice

**1. Effort flows to easy wins**
Mainland routes with good infrastructure, weather, and high flight frequency are far easier to improve. Management attention, maintenance priority, and operational resources naturally concentrate there.

**2. Resource cannibalization**
Crew, spare parts, and aircraft that previously buffer-covered island route delays may be redeployed to hit the overall target. Island routes, already logistically harder, become more fragile.

**3. Metric gaming via schedule changes**
Airlines commonly pad schedules to improve on-time rates. Island routes may get *unfavorable* schedule changes — less frequent departures, consolidated flights, or timing shifts — that technically preserve the metric while worsening real-world delivery timing.

**4. Cancellations are invisible**
A cancelled flight often isn't counted as a "late departure." Consolidated or cancelled island flights could eliminate recorded delays while leaving hospitals without supplies entirely.

---

### The Deeper Problem: Goodhart's Law

> *"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."*

Management was handed one number (96% → 98%) with no weighting for route criticality. Rational actors optimized for that number. The system delivered exactly what was measured, not what was needed.

---

### What a Better Metric System Would Include

- **Route-stratified targets** (island routes tracked separately with higher reliability requirements)
- **Critical cargo delivery rates** as a distinct KPI
- **Cancellation-adjusted metrics** (not just departure timing)
- **Penalty weighting** for medical/essential service routes
- **Frequency maintenance guarantees** for remote communities

---

### The Lesson

This scenario is a variation of **Simpson's Paradox** meets **Goodhart's Law**: the aggregate trend can point in the opposite direction of every important subgroup trend. A single overall on-time rate is a fundamentally inappropriate metric for a service with heterogeneous criticality. The 96% and 98.2% figures are both true — and both misleading without knowing *where* the performance is occurring.
